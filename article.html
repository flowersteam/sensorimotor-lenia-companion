Learning Sensorimotor Agency
in Cellular Automata

Finding robust self-organizing "agents" with gradient descent and curriculum learning: individuality, self-maintenance and sensori-motricity within a cellular automaton environment

Interactive Demo

Reproduce in a Notebook

  • Press play to begin
  • Place creature by clicking on the dark screen with Creature mode selected (note that the multi creature setting was not seen during training)
  • Now place obstacles by selecting Wall dot and then clicking or dragging on the screen
  • You can erase by selecting the Eraser mode and then clicking or dragging on the screen
  • You can choose the brush size to draw bigger obstacles, add more matter, clear bigger areas, etc.
  • To zoom, select zoom mode, select zoom magnitude with the slider and then click where you want to zoom.
    Keep dragging to follow the creature
  • For the attractor, click and drag for a short distance attractor (Still experimental, works best with creature 2)
  • You can upload images with the "Choose File" button, but we advise you to use small images (as big ones will make large grid that are computationally heavy)
  • The demo displays a differentiable continuous cellular automaton (CA) system where an emerging "creature" (yellow) is the result of local rules applied on the whole grid. The blue elements are obstacles (and are also part of the CA system). The rules leading to these very robust creatures were obtained using gradient descent, diversity search and curriculum learning.
  • Press play to begin (The demo won't work on phone)
  • Choose the type of creature on the left (each creature morphology is the result of different rules in the CA)
  • Choose predefined environments at the bottom
  • Draw obstacles by clicking on the canvas
  • More options available at Advanced Options

    Videos

    Video S1: Sensorimotor agents
    Different agents (yellow) emerging from rules obtained by the IMGEP. The agents display sensorimotor capabilities: they are robust and react to perturbations by the obstacles (blue). The rightmost video shows the system with a different colormap to highlight the differences in activity in the agent as a response to perturbations (obstacles shown in black).

    Video S2: Random search
    100 random parameters trials in Lenia (one trial per square). We use the 1-channel and 10-rules variant of Lenia is used, with 13 parameters per rule such that the parameter space is \(\sim \) 130-dimensional. We observe that a lot of random search trials lead to death or explosion of the mass. Only a small portion of these trials lead to stable spatially localized patterns, and even fewer produce moving patterns (actually none in this example).

    Video S3: Orbium, a moving agent from the original Lenia paper, is fragile to external perturbations
    (Left) Orbium, the first moving agent discovered in Lenia (from the original Lenia paper) reminiscent of the "glider" in Conway's Game of Life. (Middle-Right) Collisions between several orbiums leading to death/explosion. This shows the fragility of the orbium to external perturbations.

    Video S4: Orbium perturbed by obstacles
    The orbium also dies from perturbations by obstacles.

    Video S5: Agents obtained by the different exploration methods
    100 patterns passing the agency test (see paper) discovered by the different exploration methods (from left to right): random search, IMGEP search and handmade search (i.e. discoveries from the original Lenia papers).     Video S7 : Illustration of the quantitative generalization tests
    Examples of robustness tests that we performed to quantitatively evaluate the generalization capabilities of the discovered moving agents (here shown for 2 moving agents obtained by the IMGEP) to different out-of-distribution parametrized perturbations. Note that we display only a subset of the values tested for every quantitative test.

    Qualitative Evaluation

    Video S8: Out of distribution obstacles - Different shapes
    Test of a moving agent (obtained by IMGEP) on varying obstacle shapes that were not seen during training.

    Video S9: Out of distribution obstacles - Maze
    Test of a moving agent to "maze" like obstacles.

    Video S10: Out of distribution obstacles - "Bullet" like obstacles
    Test of a moving agent to a "bullet" like environment with fast small moving obstacles.

    Video S11: Individuality preservation
    Example of moving agents (obtained by IMGEP) able to maintain their individuality despite colliding with other agents on the grid. Contrary to the orbium pattern shown in Video S3, they do not merge nor collapse from the collision.

    Video S12: Reproduction
    For some moving agents, under specific conditions, we observe that the collision of two agents can lead to the self-organization of a third agent (each with its own individuality).

    Video S13: Attraction
    Example of moving agents attracting each other while still maintaining their own individuality.

    Video S14: Asynchronous updates
    Testing a moving agent with asynchronous updates. Each cell is updated with a certain probability at each step leading to cells being asynchronously updated.

    Video S15: Scaling the agents down
    Testing a moving agent with reduced size (i.e. with fewer cells, see paper). The scaled-down agents appear to behave similarly than the normal size ones (maintaining the same shape and sensorimotor capabilities), despite having fewer cells to perform the computation.

    Video S16: Morphological computation
    In this example, we pause the simulation and remove some cells of a moving agent. As a response to this alteration of the structure, the moving agent changes direction, regrow itself and moves away. This video illustrates the fact that the macro agent senses perturbations of its structure and respond to it by a morphological growth.

    Video S17: External control
    In this example, we introduce an attractive element in another channel (shown in cyan). The rule that controls how this external element influences the agent channel (yellow) has been learned to foster attraction, and we display the resulting behavior here. The moving agent is effectively attracted toward this newly introduced component. By controlling the position of this external element, we can dynamically steer the direction of the moving agent.

    Video S18: Robustness to initialization
    In these examples, we test the robustness of the learned rule in self-organizing an agent from various initial patterns. We replace the original learned initial pattern by: (left) a disk with gradient activation, (middle) a large disk (much larger than an agent), (right-top) a larger disk with gradient activation, (right-bottom) a larger disk with uniform activation. Some of these initializations lead to the robust emergence of one or several agents, while others lead to the collapse of the pattern.

    Video S21: Visualizing the role of the different learned rules
    To try to visualize and interpret thes role of the different learned rules (10 per discovered agent), we display the growth (in the magma colormap) of each of the 10 rules in the exterior videos. The inner top video shows the standard simulation. The inner bottom video displays the weighted sum of the growth of all the rules in grey level (1 being white and 0 being black), showing what is added to the current state of the learnable channel.
